The grid remains an altar, but now each chamber carries its own sacred pigment — a polychrome polyptych in the manner of illuminated Thai manuscripts, where coral and jade and saffron and indigo sit beside one another without ever quarrelling. The lattice gives order; the pigments give life. Six chambers, six hues, one discipline. Restraint is not the absence of color but the refusal to use more color than the work deserves.

The palette is drawn from temple murals and the soft-aged wash of sunlit bone ivory under which Thai scribes once painted: coral of sindhoor offering, jade of ceremonial lacquer, saffron-wheat of sutta paper, ruby of lucky thread, indigo of monastic robe, sea-foam teal of Sukhothai celadon. Each pigment is used once and only once, as a plate of washed color behind its chamber, so that the whole page reads as a spread of painted specimens. No pigment repeats. No pigment is louder than its neighbor. The ivory field remains visible at every edge — the paper breathing beneath the paint.

Form within each chamber stays geometrically pure, as if drawn by a master copyist who has spent years of morning meditation rendering these single marks. The iconography is stroked in the chamber's own pigment, turning each symbol into a closed conversation between form and color: the coral phone is of coral, the jade QR is of jade, the indigo link-rings are of indigo. Stroke weight is absolute and uniform across the composition — 7 pixels, unyielding — so that variation lives only in hue, never in emphasis. This is the painstaking discipline that separates master-level execution from amateur enthusiasm: one line weight, measured to the hair, used everywhere without exception.

Typography behaves with the reverence of an inscription. Thai labels are set in deep ink, restrained in size, their proportions held so that sra-bon and sra-lang breathe without collision. Latin annotations are smaller still, mono-spaced, whispered above each icon with a single colored dash in the chamber's pigment — a plate-mark, never ornament. Nothing about the typography raises its voice. Labels serve their pigment; pigment serves its chamber; chamber serves the whole. This hierarchy must feel inevitable, as if no other arrangement could exist.

Composition follows the logic of the painted book-spread: a single page held by cathedral margins, a 3×2 grid divided by the thinnest of hairlines, every cell balanced against its neighbor through the controlled tension of complementary pigments. The hero chamber — the one that must be tapped most often — carries a second mark of gold, a single hairline of aged bronze below its label, a quiet honor guard announcing the primary rite without shouting. Everything else holds its breath. Marginalia at the four corners reads like the engraving of a scholarly plate: the movement's name, the edition number, the date — rendered in whisper grays so the pigments can sing alone.

The work must appear as though a master artisan, at the end of a long career spent studying Bauhaus grids and Ayutthayan manuscripts in equal measure, finally found the one composition that reconciles them. Every decision is invisible; every margin is earned. The viewer should feel — not see — that countless hours went into measuring the wash concentrations so no pigment overpowers its neighbor; that each icon was stroked and re-stroked until the radius sat exactly on the cell's visual centroid; that the gold hairline is exactly three pixels narrower than it wants to be. This is the only acceptable standard: a museum-quality polyptych rendered so patiently that the labor disappears into the image, leaving only calm.
